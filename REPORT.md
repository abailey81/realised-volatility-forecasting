# A Machine Learning Approach to Volatility Forecasting — Replication and Critical Discussion

*Replication of Christensen, Siggaard & Veliyev (2023), "A Machine Learning Approach to Volatility Forecasting", Journal of Financial Econometrics 21(5), 1680–1727. Out-of-sample on AAPL, AMZN and JPM 5-minute data, 2016–2024. Main text is ≤3 pages; all tables, figures, robustness checks and code excerpts are in the Appendix.*

---

## 1. Problem and the original paper

Realised variance (RV) is the ex-post measure of a day's return variation — the sum of squared intraday returns (Andersen & Bollerslev 1998; Barndorff-Nielsen & Shephard 2002). It feeds risk management, option pricing and portfolio allocation, and unlike returns it is strongly predictable because volatility is persistent. The field benchmark is the Heterogeneous Autoregressive model (HAR; Corsi 2009): a linear regression of RV on its daily, weekly (5-day) and monthly (22-day) lagged averages. Four parameters approximate the long memory of volatility remarkably well, which is why decades of richer models struggle to beat it (Hansen & Lunde 2005).

Christensen, Siggaard & Veliyev (2023, "CSV") ask whether machine learning (ML) beats HAR at forecasting daily RV, and where any gain originates. They run a horse-race of 22 models: the HAR family (HAR, LogHAR, LevHAR, SHAR, HARQ), regularised regression (Ridge, Lasso, Elastic Net, adaptive- and post-Lasso), tree ensembles (bagging, random forest, gradient boosting) and feed-forward neural networks (four geometric-pyramid architectures, each as a single network NN¹ and a 10-of-100 ensemble NN¹⁰). Two information sets are compared: **M_HAR** (the three RV lags only) and **M_ALL** (M_HAR plus nine macro/firm predictors — implied volatility, an earnings dummy, momentum, dollar-volume, VIX, EPU, Hang Seng, ADS and the 3-month rate). On 29 Dow Jones constituents over 2001–2017 they conclude: ML is competitive with and beats the HAR lineage; the gain is small and ML-class-specific on M_HAR but clear on M_ALL (Elastic Net ≈ −8%, random forest ≈ −10%, best NN ≈ −11% at the one-day horizon); the gain **grows with the forecast horizon**, with random forests leading at one month (≈40% MSE reduction). Significance is judged with Diebold–Mariano (DM; 1995) tests and the Hansen–Lunde–Nason Model Confidence Set (MCS; 2011); interpretability via Accumulated Local Effects (ALE; Apley & Zhu 2020). Their reading of the source of the gain is that ML better approximates the long-memory persistence of RV and extracts incremental signal from the extra predictors.

We replicate the full methodology on the three stocks with available 5-minute data (AAPL, AMZN, JPM), 2016–2024 — a window seven years later than CSV that contains the March-2020 COVID shock and the 2022 regime. AAPL and JPM are in CSV's cross-section (a sanity check); AMZN joined the DJIA only in 2020, so it is genuine out-of-sample-stock evidence. We do not target exact numerical replication; we target a sound, transparent implementation and a critical comparison.

## 2. Implementation and replication

**Data.** From 1-minute bars we restrict to the regular 09:30–16:00 session, apply a Barndorff-Nielsen et al. (2009)-style outlier filter, resample to 5-minute returns *within* each trading day (no overnight returns), and compute RV, the signed semivariances RV±, and realised quarticity RQ (Appendix A1). The sample is ≈2,260 trading days per stock; the chronological split is 70/10/20 (train/validation/test), matching CSV.

**Models.** All 22 CSV models are implemented behind one `Forecaster` interface (Appendix A2–A3). The HAR family is closed-form OLS; LogHAR carries the Jensen back-transform correction; HARQ uses the Bollerslev–Patton–Quaedvlieg (2016) insanity filter (clip to the in-sample range). Regularised models use a 100-point log-λ grid (CSV use 1,000; the optimum is interior, so the coarser grid is immaterial — Appendix A7). Bagging/RF use Breiman–Cutler defaults; gradient boosting is validation-tuned. The NNs run on scikit-learn `MLPRegressor` (PyTorch was unavailable): this means ReLU rather than leaky-ReLU and **no dropout**, so we lean on early stopping as the regulariser — a deliberate substitution for the dropout the backend cannot provide. Following CSV (p.1691) any negative variance forecast is replaced by the in-sample minimum RV, applied uniformly.

**Estimation scheme.** The HAR family is refit daily on a sliding window (rolling), exactly as CSV. For tractability the regularised, tree and NN models are estimated once on train+validation (fixed-window). CSV instead roll the regularised models and the trees and fix only the NNs; our fixed-window choice for the trees is the main methodological deviation and we return to its consequences in §4. The validation set selects hyperparameters; the test set is untouched until evaluation. Features are standardised on training statistics; targets are aligned so that the daily lag RV_{t-1} predicts RV_t (a one-step-ahead forecast).

**Evaluation.** MSE is the primary loss (as in CSV). We add QLIKE (Patton 2011) as a noise-robust complement — CSV report MSE only. Pairwise accuracy uses DM with Newey–West HAC variance and the Harvey–Leybourne–Newbold small-sample correction; the joint comparison uses the HLN MCS (Tmax statistic, moving-block bootstrap, 10,000 replications) at α ∈ {0.25, 0.10, 0.05}. ALE with 40 quantile bins gives correlation-robust variable importance. Beyond CSV we add four checks (Appendix A8–A11): realised-kernel volatility, Mincer–Zarnowitz forecast efficiency, bootstrap confidence intervals, a multiple-testing correction, a COVID-split, and a 2-state regime analysis. Substitutions are documented: implied volatility is proxied by VIX (CSV use licensed OptionMetrics), and the earnings dummy is incomplete pre-2018. Thirty-seven unit tests cover the critical path.

## 3. Results

We select three results as most important: the headline M_ALL comparison at the one-day horizon; how it changes with horizon; and the statistical-significance picture. We justify the selection because these are the three claims CSV lead with.

| 1 | AMZN | 1.000 | 0.970 | 0.881 | 0.963 | 1.140 | 0.879 | **0.861** |

At **h=1 on M_ALL**, the best model beats HAR by ≈9–14% on every stock (AAPL 10.9%, AMZN 13.9%, JPM 8.8%; NN ensembles lead — NN2¹⁰/NN3¹⁰ at 0.86–0.91 — with Lasso close at 0.88–0.95). This reproduces CSV's headline magnitude (their best NN ≈ −11%) and ordering (NNs best at h=1, regularisation helping, bagging the weakest tree). On the apples-to-apples **M_HAR** set (Appendix A3) the gain collapses to a few percent and only the NN ensembles beat HAR, with trees losing and regularisation at par — again matching CSV.

The **horizon** picture diverges sharply and is our most informative result. CSV find ML's edge *grows* with horizon, random forests leading at h=22. We find the opposite for trees: at h=22 the fixed-window trees collapse (bagging 7–14× HAR, RF 3–5×), while **LogHAR dominates the HAR family** (0.63–0.67 on AAPL/JPM) and the NN ensembles stay competitive (0.72–1.25). The §4 discussion explains why.

**Significance is thin and loss-dependent.** At h=1, the MCS at 90% retains 20–21 of 22 models on every stock — HAR is never eliminated (Appendix A5). One-sided DM tests find only 0–3 models per stock significantly beat HAR at 5% (typically LogHAR, post-/Lasso, one NN), and under a Holm/BH multiple-testing correction almost nothing survives at h=1 (Appendix A8). Under **QLIKE**, the ML advantage largely disappears: HAR-X, Ridge, Elastic Net and the NNs all post QLIKE ratios above 1 on most stocks (Appendix), i.e. the edge is concentrated in MSE, which weights high-volatility days, and fades under a loss that weights proportional errors at low volatility. Figure 1 (MCS inclusion bars, Appendix A5) visualises the permissiveness.

## 4. Critical comparison and discussion

The replication matches CSV where the data allow — LogHAR is the strongest HAR variant, NN ensembles are best at h=1, regularisation helps on M_ALL — so the substantive contribution is the critique. Five points, each tied to a financial, econometric or ML reason; the full development with page/table references is in Appendix A6 / `CRITIQUE.md`.

**(i) The "machine learning" headline is mostly information set and regularisation, not nonlinearity [ML].** CSV's own tables separate the effects their title bundles. On the apples-to-apples M_HAR set (their Table 2, h=1) only the ensembled NNs beat HAR, by ≈5%; trees *lose* (bagging +15%) and regularisation *ties* (EN 0.999). The gain appears only on M_ALL, where plain OLS on the wider set (HAR-X) already earns 3.4%, Elastic Net 8.4%, and the best NN 11.5% — so regularisation does roughly half the work and nonlinearity the rest. Our path decomposition reproduces this split (regularisation +3–5pp, deep-NN +4–18pp, trees flat-to-negative; total 9–14pp). The ML-specific nonlinearity is a minority of the headline; "regularisation on a richer information set" would describe the result more honestly.

**(ii) CSV's striking long-horizon tree result depends on the daily re-fit, not the model class [econometric/financial].** CSV roll bagging/RF daily but fix the NNs once (p.1693, conceded as "strictly disadvantageous to the NNs"), so "RF is best at h=22" (Table 7, ≈40% MSE reduction) confounds *trees* with *re-estimated daily*. We isolate the two with a controlled experiment: with all ML fixed-window the trees collapse at h=22 (bagging 7–14× HAR, RF 3–5×); refitting RF daily — CSV's scheme — roughly halves the AAPL ratio (3.04→1.55), pinning the daily re-fit, not "trees capture long memory", as the load-bearing assumption (it does not fully reach CSV's RF<HAR, evidence the result is also sample-conditional). Financially, a model that must be constantly re-estimated to survive is the one that fails at a regime change, when volatility forecasts matter most.

**(iii) The significance machinery does not support the prose [econometric].** CSV's claim is a between-means statement (lower average MSE via DM). The MCS — the test of *distinguishable* accuracy — keeps HAR in the best set: their Figure 4 retains LogHAR at a high rate, and ours retains 20–21 of 22 models at α=0.10 with HAR never eliminated. One-sided DM finds only 0–3 models per stock beat HAR at 5%, and a Holm/Benjamini–Hochberg correction (CSV apply none, despite ≈136 pairwise tests per stock) removes essentially every h=1/h=5 star. ML lowers average MSE a few percent but is not statistically distinguishable from HAR at the daily horizon.

**(iv) The edge is loss- and regime-conditional [financial].** CSV evaluate on MSE only; under QLIKE — Patton's (2011) noise-robust loss — the advantage largely vanishes (HAR-X, Ridge, Elastic Net and the NNs are all QLIKE-worse than HAR on most stocks at h=1). MSE rewards fit on a few high-variance days; QLIKE weights the many ordinary days, where HAR is hard to beat. And on a 2016–2019-train / 2020–2024-test split (the test spanning the COVID crash and 2022) the edge collapses from ≈9–14% to ≈0–2% — no model beats HAR on JPM. Since proportional accuracy and turbulent regimes are exactly what risk management cares about, the practical value of the edge is smaller than an MSE-only, calm-period comparison implies.

**(v) Long-memory interpretation and licensed-data dependence [econometric/reproducibility].** CSV attribute the gain to ML "better approximating long memory", but their evidence is *in-sample fitted* ACF (Figure 8), which cannot distinguish captured long memory from over-smoothing toward the unconditional mean — our *out-of-sample* ACF shows ML forecasts more persistent than realised RV, the over-smoothing signature. And the M_ALL gain leans on per-stock OptionMetrics implied volatility, the strongest extra predictor (Table A.5, t = 6.4) yet licensed, unreproducible, and redundant once proxied by VIX (insignificant alongside IV, t = 0.09). Where we match CSV the agreement is reassuring; where we differ, the difference is the contribution — enabled by a regime-spanning sample they could not observe.

**Further critiques (full development in `CRITIQUE.md`).** **(vi)** The comparison runs on a noisy proxy: RV estimates integrated variance with error and HARQ exists to correct it (BPQ 2016), yet the ML methods receive the same noisy regressors uncorrected — switching to the noise-robust realised kernel moves JPM h=1 MSE by ≈27%, larger than the entire ML-vs-HAR gap, so part of the "gain" is estimator noise, not model skill [econometric]. **(vii)** "Minimal tuning" is asymmetric: CSV search a 1,000-point λ grid and keep the best 10 of 100 NN seeds but leave RF at 2004 defaults — the methods that win are the most heavily searched [ML]. **(viii)** The 22-model space is narrow: every model's out-of-sample errors correlate with HAR at r ≥ 0.79 (≈0.88–0.99 for the models that beat HAR), so each winner is HAR plus a small orthogonal perturbation and its gain lives in under ~20% of error variance — which is also why combinations add little [ML]. **(ix)** "The basic HAR is superior to its offspring" (CSV p.1695) is split-dependent: their own Appendix A.1 flips it under a shorter window, so the headline ranking is conditional on the train/test design rather than structural [econometric].
